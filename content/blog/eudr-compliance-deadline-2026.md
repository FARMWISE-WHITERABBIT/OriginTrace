## Enforcement Status: Where We Are Now

The EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) entered into force on 29 June 2023. After a one-year postponement announced in late 2023, the enforcement timeline was revised:

- **Large operators and traders** — enforcement began 30 December 2024
- **Small and micro operators** — enforcement from 30 June 2025

As of 2026, EUDR is in active enforcement for all operator categories. The European Commission has made clear that there will be no further postponements. EU member state competent authorities are conducting checks, and non-compliant shipments are being stopped at borders.

If you export cocoa, coffee, palm oil, soy, rubber, cattle products, or wood products to the EU — or if your downstream buyers include EU importers — this regulation applies to your supply chain.

---

## The Core Requirements in Plain Language

The EUDR has three core requirements that every operator in the supply chain must meet:

**Deforestation-free verification** — the commodity and any derived product must have been produced on land that was not deforested after 31 December 2020. This is a hard cutoff date. Farms established by clearing forest after that date cannot be included in EUDR-compliant supply chains.

**Legal production** — production must comply with the relevant legislation of the country of production. For agricultural commodities this includes land use rights, environmental legislation, labour rights, and anti-corruption legislation in the producing country. Operators must conduct due diligence to verify legal compliance, not just deforestation status.

**Due diligence** — operators must conduct and document due diligence before placing a product on the EU market. Due diligence is a structured process: information gathering (farm-level data, geolocation, supply chain records), risk assessment, and risk mitigation. The result of the due diligence process — the due diligence statement — must be submitted to the EU Information System before placing the product on the market.

## What Data You Actually Need to Collect

The data requirements of EUDR are more specific than most exporters initially expect. Here is what you need, at the transaction level:

**Farm-level geolocation** — GPS coordinates for every plot of land where the commodity was produced. For plots up to 4 hectares, a single GPS point is accepted. For plots above 4 hectares, a polygon boundary is required. A community boundary or village-level coordinate does not satisfy this requirement.

**Farmer identification** — the name and identity of the producer for each farm in your supply chain. This does not mean KYC-level verification, but you need a traceable link from every bag of cocoa back to the farmer who grew it.

**Date of production** — when was this commodity produced? For annual crops like cocoa, this is the harvest season. This matters for demonstrating production after the 2020 forest baseline.

**Country and region of production** — country-level data is insufficient. You need the sub-national administrative region (state, province) of production for each batch.

**Supply chain chain of custody** — complete documentation of who sold what to whom at each step between the farm and the EU importer. Anonymous bulk purchases break this chain.

## The Countries Most Affected

EUDR's country risk classification directly affects how much due diligence is required for shipments from a given origin:

**High risk countries** require full due diligence — detailed farm-level data, deforestation risk assessment for each polygon, and documented risk mitigation measures. Most major cocoa and coffee producing countries in West Africa and Southeast Asia are currently classified in this tier.

**Standard risk countries** require due diligence but with somewhat less stringent verification requirements.

**Low risk countries** benefit from simplified due diligence, though the farm-level data requirements remain.

The European Commission publishes and updates the country risk list. Exporters should check the current classification for their country of origin before each shipment season.

For Nigerian exporters: Nigeria is currently in the standard or high-risk tier for cocoa and other covered commodities. Full due diligence requirements apply.

## The Due Diligence Statement: What It Is and How to Submit It

The due diligence statement (DDS) is the formal output of your compliance process. It is submitted electronically to the EU EUDR Information System before the product is placed on the EU market or exported.

The DDS contains:
- Description of the product (commodity, HS code, quantity)
- Country of production and sub-national region
- Geolocation data for all plots (GPS points or polygon coordinates)
- Your deforestation-free and legal production statements
- The supply chain information (your details and the first EU operator in the chain)

The DDS is not a one-time submission. It must be prepared and submitted for each shipment lot. If you are making ten shipments per season, you need ten DDS submissions, each linked to the specific farm-level data for that particular lot.

EU importers and traders will ask you for your DDS reference numbers. They will not be able to clear your shipments through their own due diligence process without them.

## Common Mistakes That Lead to Non-Compliance

**Relying on cooperative-level data** — EUDR requires plot-level GPS data for individual farms, not cooperative-level or aggregated regional data. A cooperative GPS location with 200 farmers' production attributed to it does not satisfy the geolocation requirement.

**Treating the DDS as a document rather than a data process** — the DDS is the output of a data collection and verification process. Filling out a DDS form without the underlying farm-level data it represents is falsification of a regulatory submission. EU authorities are checking the data, not just the form.

**Missing the pre-placement submission requirement** — the DDS must be submitted before the product is placed on the EU market, which means before it arrives at the EU border and before the sale contract is executed with an EU buyer. Submitting after arrival is non-compliant.

**Not tracking the deforestation reference dataset** — EUDR references a specific forest cover baseline as of 31 December 2020. Your compliance verification needs to check your farm polygons against this specific dataset, not generic satellite imagery or current forest cover data.

## Getting Compliant in Practice

The path to EUDR compliance runs through your field data collection process. Everything else — risk assessment, DDS generation, submission — depends on having accurate, complete, verifiable farm-level data.

The practical sequence is:

1. Audit your current supplier database — how many of your active farmers have GPS polygon data? What percentage have individual farm records as opposed to community-level records?

2. Deploy a farm mapping process — use the field visits and collection rounds you are already doing to collect GPS polygons for farms that are missing them. This is the highest-leverage single action.

3. Run each polygon through a deforestation check — identify any farms that have potential deforestation flags. Engage those farmers to understand land use history and either clear the flag or remove the farm from your supply chain.

4. Build lot-level traceability — ensure that every lot you prepare for EU buyers can be traced batch-by-batch back to the specific farms with verified polygons.

5. Generate and submit your DDS — once the data is in place, DDS generation becomes a routine step.
